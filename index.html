The problem

Why LinkedIn looks worthless (and why that's an illusion)

If you judge LinkedIn by last-click web analytics, you will kill it. In GA4 over the last year, Paid Social — which includes all LinkedIn traffic — drove just 1,231 of ~120,000 sessions (under 1%), and GA4 logged only 41 tracked key events all year across every channel. The measurement simply cannot see awareness working.

Website sessions by channel · last 12 months
Demand does not arrive wearing a LinkedIn badge. It arrives as Organic Search, Direct and branded Paid Search — the three biggest channels above — which is precisely where LinkedIn-seeded demand cashes out and gets the credit. The channels at the top are harvesting demand the channel at the bottom created.

How far ahead LinkedIn shows up · all 817 influenced accounts

Across every influenced account, the first LinkedIn touch lands a median of ~13 months before the deal is created. 92% are reached 6+ months ahead, and 60% a full year or more ahead. The pipeline showing up in the CRM today was being warmed by LinkedIn long before sales ever logged it.

A note on honesty: this is open pipeline, not won revenue — only 3 deals have closed so far, because the journeys are young and the cycle is long. The point is not "LinkedIn closed these deals." It is that LinkedIn consistently shows up first, far upstream, in the accounts that become pipeline.

Google

Proof 2 — Demand resurfacing as branded search

When awareness works, people later search for you by name. In NL Google Ads, branded search (people typing “Studytube”) pulled 23,665 clicks and 31 of 40 Google conversions for just €5,641 — while non-branded search cost €50,356 for 9 conversions. Branded demand is cheap because it was already created upstream.

LinkedIn investment (bars) vs NL branded-search clicks (line) · monthly
LinkedIn spendBranded search clicks

Branded-search clicks climbed from ~540/month to a 2,400–2,900/month plateau through the second half of 2025, in the wake of the heavy summer LinkedIn push. (March 2026 is a known branded-campaign budget pause, not a demand drop.) Given Fibbler's ~13-month first-touch-to-deal runway, this is correlation with a long delay, not a same-month claim — but the shape is the awareness flywheel.

A second efficiency signal: in the DACH high-intent search campaign, as LinkedIn built awareness in-market, the same search ads got cheaper and sharper — CTR rose from 2.3% to as high as 9.9%, and CPC fell from €8.41 to around €2.50. Awareness does not just feed branded search; it makes paid search go further too.
Why the search ads got more efficient

This is not a coincidence, it is how Google's auction works. Why CTR went up: click-through rate is mostly driven by recognition. As more of the people searching had already seen Studytube on LinkedIn, the brand felt familiar in the results page instead of unknown, so a bigger share clicked.

Why CPC then dropped: in Google's auction you do not simply pay your bid. Position is set by Ad Rank ≈ bid × Quality Score, and Quality Score is driven largely by expected click-through rate. So a rising CTR earns a higher Quality Score, which lets you win the same position for a lower cost per click. The campaign was on manual bidding, so this was the auction charging less for more relevant ads, not an algorithm quietly lowering bids. The loop: awareness → recognition → higher CTR → higher Quality Score → lower CPC. Top-of-funnel spend lowers the unit cost of bottom-of-funnel spend.

Honest caveat: some of this is also normal campaign maturation (better keywords, negative keywords, refined copy), so awareness is not the only driver. But a 4× jump in CTR and a ~3× drop in CPC, in the exact market where LinkedIn was spending heavily, is strongly consistent with brand familiarity feeding the search auction.

Geography

Two markets, two jobs — NL and DE

The paid programme runs across two very different markets. NL is the mature market where demand already exists and the job is to harvest it efficiently. DE (DACH) is the emerging market where the job is to build awareness from a low base — in a far more expensive, more competitive auction.

The model

How the channels work as one system

Each channel plays a role. Judged in isolation on last-click conversions, most look weak. Judged as a system, they hand demand down the funnel to one another.

AwarenessLinkedIn

The demand engine. Judge on reach, frequency, engagement and CPM — not clicks-to-conversion. €251.8k/yr, 7.4M impressions. Seeds the pipeline ~13 months ahead.

Always-on captureGoogle Ads

Harvests the demand others create. Branded search is the cheapest, highest-converting line (31/40 conversions for €5.6k); high-intent search catches active buyers.

Test channelBing

Low-cost incremental search coverage. Data to follow.

Retargeting / omnipresenceMeta

Keeps the brand present across the consideration window and re-engages known prospects. Data to follow.

Takeaways

The bottom line

  • LinkedIn is in front of 85% of pipeline value (€534.7k) before the deal exists. It is the demand engine, not the closer.
  • Last-click can't see it: under 1% of sessions and 41 tracked events all year. Judging LinkedIn on last-click conversions is measuring the wrong thing.
  • The demand resurfaces as branded search and direct traffic — 23,665 branded clicks and 31/40 Google conversions for €5,641, plus search ads that got cheaper as awareness grew.
  • Real ICP accounts are being reached and returning: 115 in-ICP companies engaged, 38% more than once.
  • Two markets, two jobs: NL harvests mature demand cheaply (CPM €23); DE builds a new market at a premium (CPM €82, ~3.5× NL). The high DACH price is the cost of entry, not inefficiency.
  • Treat the channels as one system. Cutting awareness to chase last-click efficiency would starve the search and direct demand that everything else converts.
